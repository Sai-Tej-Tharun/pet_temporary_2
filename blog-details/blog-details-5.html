DIY Pet Care at Home: 10 Grooming Habits Every Pet Parent Should Build

Professional grooming does wonders — but a PawSpa appointment every six weeks can only do so much if nothing happens in between. The difference between a coat that arrives matted and a coat that arrives in beautiful condition is almost entirely what the owner does at home. These ten habits take under an hour per week in total and transform your pet's health, coat, and comfort.

Why Home Care Matters

Professional groomers are not magicians. When a dog arrives with six weeks of unbrushed mats, the session becomes an hour of careful detangling or, in severe cases, a full shave-down. Neither serves the dog well. Home care bridges the gap between appointments — it maintains what a professional groom achieves and protects the investment you've made in your pet's coat health.

There's a second, equally important reason: the bond. Grooming is physical contact. It teaches your pet that being touched — paws, ears, mouth, all of it — is normal and safe. A dog or cat who is regularly handled at home is calmer at the vet, more relaxed at the groomer, and generally less anxious in novel situations.

Habits 1–3: Daily Essentials (Under 5 Minutes Total)

Habit 1: The Daily Brush

For long and curly-coated dogs and cats, a 2–3 minute brush every single day prevents the mats that accumulate overnight and during play. Use a slicker brush and always work from the ends of the coat toward the roots — never root to tip, which tightens tangles. For short-coated pets, a quick rubber brush stroke 3–4 times per week is sufficient.

Habit 2: Eye Wipe

Many breeds — especially flat-faced breeds like Pugs, French Bulldogs, Shih Tzus, and Persians — produce eye discharge daily. A damp cotton ball wiped gently from the inner corner outward removes this before it stains or causes skin fold irritation. This takes 30 seconds and prevents the chronic rust-coloured tear staining that accumulates on white and light-coloured coats.

Habit 3: Post-Walk Paw Check

After every outdoor walk, take 60 seconds to check paw pads for cuts, cracking, embedded debris, or grass seeds. Grass seeds in particular are a common seasonal emergency — they migrate through the skin and can travel to internal organs if missed early. Between the toes is the highest-risk zone. While you're there, check the fur between the pads; this area mats quickly and, when knotted, causes painful pulling with every step.

⏱️
The 3-Minute Rule

If you set a timer, you'll discover that habits 1–3 combined take under 3 minutes for a short-coated pet. Even for a long-coated breed, a thorough daily brush is rarely more than 5 minutes. The barrier is habit, not time. Stack these onto an existing routine — right after the morning walk, or while your coffee brews.

Habits 4–6: Weekly Routines (15–20 Minutes Total)

Habit 4: Ear Inspection

Once a week, gently fold back each ear and look and smell. A healthy ear is pale pink, dry, and odourless. A yeasty or sour smell, brown or black discharge, redness, or swelling means a vet call is needed — do not attempt to clean an infected ear at home, as this can push debris further into the canal. For healthy ears, a weekly wipe with a vet-approved solution and a cotton ball (not swab) removes normal wax build-up.

Habit 5: Dental Brushing

This is the most impactful and most neglected home care habit. Brushing teeth three to four times a week — ideally daily — with a pet-specific enzymatic toothpaste prevents the periodontal disease that affects 80% of adult dogs. Start by letting your pet lick the toothpaste off your finger, then progress to a finger brush, then a proper pet toothbrush. Consistency matters more than technique. Thirty seconds per quadrant is the goal.

Habit 6: Full Body Scan

Once a week, during a relaxed petting session, deliberately work your hands over your pet's entire body. Feel for new lumps, bumps, or swellings. Check the lymph nodes under the jaw, in front of the shoulder blades, behind the knees, and in the groin. Check the skin for redness, scaling, or hot spots. Run your fingers through the coat looking for flea dirt or ticks. This ritual — which your pet will love as a massage — is your best early warning system.

Invest in breed-appropriate tools — the right brush makes a 5-minute session more effective than 20 minutes with the wrong one.

Habits 7–9: Monthly Care (30–40 Minutes Total)

Habit 7: Nail Trim

Monthly nail trims for most breeds — every 3 weeks for fast-growing nails. The goal is to keep nails short enough that they don't touch the ground when the dog stands on a flat surface. Use sharp, correctly sized clippers (blunt clippers crush rather than cut, causing discomfort), work in good light, and cut small increments. End every nail session with a high-value treat and a play session. This conditions a positive association with the process over time.

Habit 8: Bath (Breed-Dependent)

Monthly bathing is appropriate for most dogs. More frequent bathing can strip natural oils and cause dry, flaky skin — less frequent bathing allows oil accumulation and skin odour. Use a diluted pet-specific shampoo, warm water, and rinse meticulously. For cats, bathing is rarely needed monthly unless they have a skin condition, are elderly, or are a hairless breed.

Habit 9: Coat Trim Maintenance

Between professional appointments, light maintenance trimming around the eyes, under the tail, between the paw pads, and around the sanitary area keeps your pet comfortable and hygienic. Blunt-tipped scissors are safest for home use around the face. For any scissor work near eyes, use rounded safety scissors and have a second person hold the dog still.

The clients whose pets arrive in the best condition are almost always the ones who've made grooming part of their daily rhythm — not a special event, but a five-minute habit after the morning walk, like making tea. When it becomes routine, it stops being a task.

— Meera Iyer, Senior Grooming Specialist, PawSpa

Habit 10: The Monthly Bonding Check

Once a month, deliberately photograph your pet from the same angles: both sides, face, and tail area. These photos serve two purposes. First, they create a visual record you can compare against — changes in weight, coat condition, posture, or lumps are easy to miss day-to-day but obvious when comparing a photo from six months ago. Second, they capture the relationship you're building. The pets who are most comfortable at the groomer and the vet are the ones who have been handled with affection and intentionality from the start.

Building the Habit Stack

The secret to maintaining any routine is habit stacking: attaching new behaviours to existing ones. Brush immediately after the morning walk. Do the weekly ear check on Sunday evenings while watching television. Brush teeth after your own evening dental routine. Nail trim on the first Sunday of every month. When the when is decided in advance, decision fatigue disappears.

📋
Your Weekly Grooming Checklist

Daily: Brush (long coats), eye wipe, paw check. Weekly: Ear inspection, dental brushing ×3, full body scan. Monthly: Nail trim, bath, light maintenance trim, photo record. Print this and put it on the fridge for the first month until it becomes automatic.
